What are anxiety disorders?

Anxiety disorders are a group of mental disorders that cause consistent anxiety and fearful thoughts every passing day. The fearful and anxious thoughts can be overwhelming for them, but with treatments such as medication and therapy, it will become more bearable.

Six common anxiety disorders are:

Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) is an anxiety disorder characterized by unprovoked worry and anxiety. Someone with GAD will often worry about everything, even something as normal as walking down a hall. These worries interfere with one’s everyday life and make it difficult to complete everyday tasks.

Panic Disorders

Panic disorder is an anxiety disorder where people experience unexpected and recurring panic attacks. They start to worry about future panic attacks and fear that something bad might happen at the next panic attack. Just because one gets a panic attack doesn’t mean they have panic disorder though. Some things can trigger a panic attack, such as stress or fear. However, one has a panic disorder when panic attacks occur for no clear reason, which drives fear into the affected person.

Panic Attacks

Panic attacks are sudden rushes of intense fear that appear when there is no danger, and trigger physical reactions such as:

  1. Rapid heartbeat
  2. Shaking or trembling
  3. Nausea or dizziness
  4. Sweating
  5. Numbness or tingling sensations
  6. Chills or hot flashes
  7. Feelings of being choked

Post Traumatic Stress Disorder

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) is an anxiety disorder that develops after witnessing or experiencing a traumatic event. This leads to symptoms such as flashbacks, nightmares, or anxiety about the past event.

More symptoms include:

  1. Re-experiencing the event through nightmares and flashbacks
  2. Avoiding places, people, or activities that are reminders of the traumatic event
  3. Distress or physical reactions to things that are reminders of the event
  4. Negative thoughts about world, others, or self
  5. Inability to remember important aspects of the event
  6. Trouble sleeping

Obsessive Compulsive Disorder

Obsessive compulsive disorder (OCD) is an anxiety disorder where people have unwanted, recurring thoughts or urges that cause discomfort and distress (obsessions), causing them to repeat a specific action to bring temporary relief (compulsions). The repetitive actions can cause great distress and interfere with a person’s daily life. Most people with OCD know or suspect that the obsessions are not true, however, the compulsive activities are still done regardless.

Generally, the obsessions revolve around a loved one or their self being harmed unless they complete a specific action repeatedly. The compulsive actions involve:

  1. Washing or cleaning
  2. Ordering or arranging
  3. Mental actions
  4. Checking or double-checking
  5. Counting, tapping, touching, or rubbing

Social Anxiety Disorder

Social anxiety disorder is a mental disorder in which one feels an overwhelming amount of stress and discomfort during social situations. Social contact that may appear normal to others-such as keeping eye contact or making small conversation- would make one feel uncomfortable, to the point of avoiding social situations altogether. Social anxiety disorder varies amongst different people. One person could be able to talk one-on-one, but be unable to go into a crowded room. Another might be good at talking in a small group, but freeze up in a big group of strangers.

A few general symptoms include:

  1. Fear of being judged, embarrassed, or humiliated
  2. Dizziness
  3. Stomach trouble
  4. Difficulty breathing
  5. Fear of offending others or being in the center of attention

Phobic Disorders

Phobic disorders (Phobias) are irrational fears of a specific situation, object, animals, or activity. One with a phobia will feel intense fear towards the specific situation or thing, and will attempt to avoid it at all costs. Though most recognize the irrationality of their phobias, usually the thought is already enough to create anxiety and fear.

When faced with the specific phobia, one might feel:

  1. Fast heartbeaat
  2. Difficulty breathing
  3. Trembling or shaking
  4. Sweating
  5. Overwhelming anxiety or fear
  6. Urge to escape
